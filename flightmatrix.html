American Airlines Vacations

Switchfly Project:
A New Enhanced Air Shopping Experience

Overview

Background

Switchfly’s Loyalty & Shopping application is a white label software that allows airlines and credit card companies to sell travel packages to consumers. Long-standing client American Airlines came to Switchfly with a request to create a flight shopping experience for their vacation package website that would be similar to their parent site, but would allow for the display of additional travel classes and product fare offerings.

As the sole designer for this project, I oversaw the end-to-end design process, where I conducted research, user testing, designed mockups, created prototypes, iterated based on product management/client feedback, and worked directly with engineering, outlining specifications for the UI.

Project Goals

Taking into consideration the client’s needs and our own business goals, the main objectives of this project were to:

  1. Create a new UI structure within our platform that could accommodate multiple product offerings in order to drive upsell opportunities for the airline
  2. Maintain a clear and easy shopping experience to promote customer retention and increase conversions rates
  3. Create a differentiated product within the travel industry marketplace, inciting client adoption and attracting new clients to generate new business and revenue

Discover

During the research phase of this project, I conducted an extensive competitor analysis, reviewing 10 other airlines websites to collect and compare data of those with similar multiple travel class and fare display structures.

Key Takeaways

From my competitor analysis, I found that:

  • Most competitors usually laid out travel class and fare options in a horizontal, grid-like format.
  • Many had strong messaging hierarchies, where price, time, and airport code tended to stand out.
  • I observed in what ways we could incorporate a more modern look and feel to our current app. I noticed that many did this through the thoughtful use of standardized spacing and color, as well as careful detail to font styles.

Define

From the onset of this project, I worked very closely with the product manager to understand all the requirements and use cases requested by our client. I also kept in close contact with our engineers to understand the technical constraints and accessibility considerations. Our product manager created a business requirement document detailing important key functionality which included:

  • Desktop and mobile web templates to support the flight shopping experience
  • The ability for the UI to support up to 5 cabin classes and 5 fare options
  • Ensure the experience incorporated industry best practices for accessibility and met the WCAG Standard at the AA Level
  • Handling different use cases that involved supporting our huge system of configurations, as well as varying states, price displays, and flight information

Prototype created in Proto.io
Link to prototype

Designing this feature for desktop was one thing, but handheld proved to be even more of a challenge. How would we be able to condense things even more, but still maintain a logical flow? I decided on a direction for handheld, but I felt it was necessary to conduct a usability test to validate if the design worked. I then followed that with a preference test to pin down the final details of the handheld design.

Testing Results

I tested my prototype on a total of 20 participants. Some of the key takeaways from testing included:
.
  • The majority of users were able to complete tasks and get through the prototype easily. The nature of the multiple fare levels was complicated, but most users were able to understand the flow.
  • In general, when users are shopping for flights, the following are the most important factors: price, times, and number of stops.
  • We learned there could be more clarity when the user opened the cabin drawer. Also, we learned that users liked seeing layover information outright.

Conclusion

Results

The feature went live on June 13, 2019 (see it in action on the AAVacations website), and it has already generated a lot of interest within our client base of other notable airline and credit card companies. Some of the things we’ll be tracking in order to gauge its success are:
  • Conversion rates — we’ll be regularly measuring how this product affects trip booking rates and how it compares to the conversion of other flight products.
  • Customer satisfaction & NPS score — we'll be looking to our client to provide us this information, as well as conduct a regular benchmark study of our own to measure these items.
  • Client adoption — our account management and product teams are already in discussions with some of our other clients about implementing this new feature. It will be exciting to see how many clients adopt it and how much new business it drives.
  • New customer acquisition — our sales team has started incorporating this product into their demos, and we’ll be keeping track of how many new clients include the feature as part of their contract.

Next Steps

If given more time on this project, I would take the following next steps:
  • Refine the responsive behavior for tablet view
  • Unify current flight cards throughout our application and see what aspects from this design can be incorporated to the other ones
  • On the mobile view, work with developers to make sure the whole flight card is clickable and opens the drawer to see the cabin options
