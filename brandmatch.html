Brand Match

Switchfly Project:
Improving the Client Branding Process
and Redesigning a System

Overview

As a white label software, Switchfly gives clients the ability to customize their interface to match the look and feel of their company's brand. In the past, clients and our own product team had complained that this process of applying brand styles took too long and involved too much back & forth. One of my first projects at Switchfly was to improve and redesign that process.

The Challenge

Previously, there wasn't much documentation around how Brand Match worked. Clients were provided a Microsoft Word document, which only listed a few styles that could be changed and included an outdated version of Switchfly's application in its default styles.



(Previous Microsoft Word documents)


This caused a lot of confusion and didn't really give clients a good sense of how styles would be applied. There needed to be an easier way to translate what elements and styles clients could customize, as well as a better visual of how their styles would look on the application.

My Role

I was the sole designer for this project. I worked with a product manager and several engineers. I oversaw research, user experience and interface design, and process improvements for this project.

Project Goals

  1. Improve a broken process to reduce frustration amongst our clients.
  2. Create a tool that would help clients better visualize their brand styles on our application and design system, as well as give them a better understanding of what styles they could change.
  3. Reduce time & effort for our developers by providing them with documentation and more clear-cut specifications to implement clients' brand styles.

Discover

Audit

I started the project by going through each page of our application and inspecting each element to see how styles were being applied, documenting those values every step of the way.

Next, I cross-referenced those values against our standard app theming scss file, which is a code document that contains all the variables used to style a client’s website.

Following my audit, I worked together with one of our developers to confirm which variables from our app theme file matched with which component in our app. Next, I sifted through our client websites and each of their app theme files to determine which items were commonly overridden. From there, I listed and defined what items we wanted to highlight for our clients to be able to override out-of-the-box. This list became the basis of my updated documentation.

Tool Exploration

Going into the project, I knew I wanted to do away with the Microsoft Word document and build a tool that allowed us to be able to create up-to-date documentation, as well as generate mockups to help clients visualize how brand styles would be applied. We explored multiple design tools, but ultimlately chose to move forward with Sketch because of its powerful symbols feature.

Define

Process

I worked with a product manager from our core platform team to flesh out how to improve the process and better define the UX team's involvement throughout each stage of the process.

Design

Iteration

The Brand Match Sketch Tool went through multiple iterations until we came across a format that worked. The tool had to be intuitive enough that any designer on our team could take the Sketch file and understand how to use it, and possibly a designer from the client’s side, as well.

In Action

Basically, the Sketch file is divided 3 main parts:
  1. Brand specifications guide - a document consisting of different artboards, where the designer can see all the symbols and elements from our app that could be changed.
  2. Symbols page - the symbols section where the designer actually updates the style values (ie. hex values, font sizes, etc.).
  3. Page previews - mockups of key pages from our app in desktop and mobile views, which are built out of the symbols from the other sections.

Output

After the designer applies the client's styles to Brand Match Sketch Tool, the following items will get generated:

  • Powerpoint document - that outlines how styles and overrides are mapped to the app for both the client and our own developers to refer to
  • Mockups of key pages - from our application with the client’s styles applied, which gives them a preview of their themed version of the site

Deliver

Ultimately, the end deliverable of this project was a Sketch file that would be re-used by our team everytime a client requested to have their version of the app go through a Brand Match. In addition to the Brand Match Sketch Tool, as mentioned earlier, I also worked together with one of our platform’s lead product managers to further define the UX design team’s role in the process.

The Brand Match process has become a product initiative that I continue to lead and own. To this day, I've overseen the process for more than 8 client implementations, including ones for some of our big-name clients, like JetBlue, Japan Airlines, Marriott, JP Morgan Chase, and Copa Airlines.

Conclusion

Results

Overall, the Brand Match process improvements made an impact through the following ways:

  • A more streamlined Brand Match process overall.
  • Cut the development time and effort down from an average of 1400 hours to 170 hours.
  • Reduced the amount of design time from 2 days (~16 hours) down to ~4 hours.
  • Significantly increased client satisfaction. See client feedback below.

Some of the feedback we've received from clients:

  • “This is great. This is definitely leaps and bounds better than what we had to go through.”
  • “We don't want to have to put together mock-ups and set the wrong expectations with our own clients. Switchfly’s mock-ups will be better to make sure everything shown is feasible."
  • "Excited to show this to new clients."
