Instashop

Designlab UX Academy Project:
Responsive Web Design for a Grocery App

Overview

Background

Instashop is a fictitious brick and mortar grocery franchise based in the United States. The investors of Instashop believe there is a big market out there for online shopping and want to evaulate its viablity within the space. As the designer, I was tasked to understand who the customers are and problems in the current space in order to build a product that improves upon the online shopping experience and position the company to be a major player within the market.

Discover

I first created a research plan that centered around understanding the target audience and what their current shopping habits look like, as well as their motivations and frustrations when it comes to online shopping. I conducted user interviews with 3 participants and distributed an online survey.

Primary Research Findings

The biggest frustrations participants had with grocery shopping included having to deal with crowds and stores not having products that they wanted. When it came to choosing a grocery store to shop in, the following were the most important factors as voted by participants: variety of products, good prices, distance from their house, and quality of products. Free and fast delivery appeared to be the most important factor when it came to what would make it easier for them to do their grocery shopping online.

Competitive Analysis

To arrive at a deeper understanding of the problem space, I conducted a competitive analysis to see how others are trying to solve the problem. I evaulated the following competitors: AmazonFresh, Instacart, and Good Eggs.

Define

Based on user research and data, I came up with a persona that represented the target demographic for Instashop — the young, tech-savvy, busy working professional.

Persona - the young busy professional

Information Architecture

In order to help organize content and categories within the site, I conducted a card sorting exercise with users. Based on results, I created the sitemap below to show the relationship between different pieces of content within the site.

Following the sitemap, a user flow was created to illustrate how a user would complete each of the following tasks: arrives to homepage, finds items to buy; adds items to cart; and checks out, signs up in the process of checking out.

Design

Wireframes

Building off my sitemap and user flow chart, I drafted up some low-fidelity wireframes in Sketch that incorporated elements of common design patterns and illustrated the overall layout of the app.

Test

Remote Testing

Five tests were conducted remotely — 2 carried out through the Verify app: 1) a click test to see how users navigated through the app and 2)a memory test to see what users remembered about the item detail page; 3 carried out through UsabilityHub: the first two similar to ones done through Verify and an additional preference test.

Usability Testing

I combined several of my wireframes and created a prototype first using the MarvelApp, then a more detailed one through InVision. 3 participants were asked to complete tasks centered around creating an account, adding an item to the cart, and checking out.

User testing results revealed that I needed to make the following adjustments for my app: 1) clarify headlines and copy, especially within the checkout process, 2)make the "ADD" button more prominent on the item detail page, and 3) place the zip code input further down the sign up process.

Deliver

Brand Style

For the visual style, I wanted to take on a direction that was a little more fun, fresh, and unconventional. For the logo, I implemented a gif of multiple icons to emphasize the variety of product Instashop offered in a quirky kind of way. As a back up, I planned to use the first frame in the gif, which is the logo with the orange icon. Additionally, I went with the bold, geometric Futura font and a vibrant color palette, as well as stylized photography, to drive home the fun and fresh direction .

Final Handoff

Pulling from my brand styles and wireframes, as well as using the standard responsive design pattern, I created layouts for the different viewports — desktop, tablet, and mobile — as demonstrated by the homepage mockups below.

Conclusion

Lessons Learned

This was a big project that taught me a lot about each step of the UX Design process. Most importantly, this project taught me the value of user research. For example, before I conducted my research, I had some presumptions about how the target demographic would look like. However, as I went through the research process, I discovered that the target audience was quite different than what I had originally drawn up from my provisional persona.

Next Steps

If given more time on this project, I would take the following next steps:
  • Explore and create flows for other features including saving/favoriting an item and looking up a recipe
  • On the item detail page, A/B test a version showing a grid of personal recommondations of products vs. a grid of recipes
  • Create a quiz during sign up that would capture user preferences in order to help inform personalized recommendations
