Instashop

Designlab UX Academy Project:
An Online Grocery Shopping App

Overview

Background

Instashop is a fictitious grocery franchise based in the United States. Over the years, market shares have been decreasing most likely due to the increase in online grocery ordering & delivery products. The investors of Instashop believe there is a big market out there for online shopping and are determined to be onle of the major players in this page.

As the designer, I was tasked to understand who the customers are and problems in the current space in order to build a product that improves upon their online shopping experience.

Discover

I first created a research plan that centered around understanding the target audience and what their current shopping habits look like, as well as their motivations and frustrations when it comes to online shopping. I conducted user interviews with 3 participants and distributed an online survey.

Primary Research Findings

Highlighted below are key insights from my primary research:
  • The biggest frustrations participants had with grocery shopping included having to deal with crowds and stores not having products that they wanted.
  • When it came to choosing a grocery store to shop in, the following were the most important factors as voted by participants: variety of products, good prices, distance from their house, and quality of products.
  • Free and fast delivery appeared to be the most important factor when it came to what would make it easier for them to do their grocery shopping online.

Competitive Analysis

To arrive at a deeper understanding of the problem space, I conducted a competitive analysis to see how others are trying to solve the problem. I evaulated the following competitors: AmazonFresh, Instacar, and Good Eggs.

Define

Based on user research and data, I came up with a persona that represented the target demographic for Instashop — the young, tech-savvy, busy working professional. In order to understand and communicate the problems and mindset of our users, I created an empathy map that centered around this persona.

Design

Wireframes

With my new feature's site structure in place, I used my personas and Spotify's existing design patterns as a guide to draw up my initial wireframes in Sketch. I focused on the key screens relevant to the flows I carved out in my sitemap. During this time, I explored a couple of different variations of the Mixer interface.

Test

For the final deliverable of my project, I wanted to hand off high-fidelity wireframes and prototype. In order to get there, I planned out a couple of rounds of wireframe iterations and tests that would allow me to arrive on the best solution.
I conducted a couple of tests on UsabilityHub to get a gauge on the direction of my initial wireframes:
  1. Navigation Test - I conducted this test to see if the screens I created for adding a song to a playlist made sense. Overall, this test saw 16 responses with a high success rate throughout each of the 5 steps.
  2. Preference Test - I wanted to see which design users would prefer — a navigation bar with links spelled out or with icons representing them. Results showed that a majority of users preferred the navigation bar with links spelled.
Results of preference test

Iteration & Prototype

I took the results from my first round of testing and began building the high-fidelity wireframes and prototype. I saw this project as a good opportunity to learn how to use the Framer tool and used it to create my prototype.

Usability Testing Results

I tested my prototype with 3 participants. Tasks included creating a new "Mixer" group chat, adding a song to a "Mixer" playlist, sending a chat message, and liking a song. Overall, there was a high completion rate of 95%, which indicated that the design and flow were headed in the right direction. Most confusion seemed to lie in the limitations of the prototype and initial understanding of the new feature itself.

Conclusion

Lessons Learned

This was one of the first projects during my time in Designlab, where I had to design something within an already existing system. Initially, when I was thinking through the UI, I tried to pull in different ideas from social apps. However, I found it much easier once I remembered to return to Spotify's existing design patterns. It was helpful to have these brand, UI, and interactive elements already in place. This allowed me to focus on other things like learning a new tool — Framer.

When it came to creating my prototype, the learning curve for using Framer was steeper than I thought. In the end, I was able to create a working prototype that transitioned between screens, but I didn't get as far as I had hoped in being able to create custom interactions. Though, it was still a good learning experience.

Next Steps

If given more time on this project, I would take the following next steps:
  • Build addtional custom interactions within the high-fidelity Framer prototype
  • Further optimize the flow to create a new Mixer group through A/B testing different versions of the screens involved
  • Work on an onboarding flow to give users a better idea of how the feature works
